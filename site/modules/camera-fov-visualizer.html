What each camera actually sees

Each panel below is a simplified frame for that camera: field markers within its cone are drawn at the correct bearing, and a gold band marks the slice of the frame that another camera's cone also reaches at that camera's typical operating depth. A gold ring around a marker means that specific object is visible to more than one camera — useful when you're deciding which camera should be the "primary" for a given target.

Overview

Why camera placement matters

A single camera on a robot only sees a wedge of the field — how wide that wedge is depends on its field of view (FOV), and how far it reaches is your effective detection range. Adding a second (or third) camera can widen your coverage, cover a blind spot behind the first camera, or give you redundant eyes on the same target — but only if you place it correctly. Mount two cameras too close together and pointed the same way and you've basically built one camera twice; toe them outward and you can lose the overlap you need for triangulation or hand-off between cameras entirely.

Objective

Design a two-camera (or more) layout

Try it: start from the default two-camera rig and toe the cameras outward (increase the angle magnitude) to see coverage width grow but the near-field blind gap open up right in front of the robot. Then toe them inward instead — the blind gap closes, but you give up total field width. Add a third camera facing straight back (angle near ±180° isn't supported here, but try a wide FOV near 150°) to cover the space behind the robot. There's no single right answer — the sliders let you feel the trade-off before you ever drill a mounting hole.

Reference

Controls & terms

Position
Where the camera sits along the robot's mounting baseline, in inches left(-)/right(+) of center. The distance between two cameras is the difference between their positions.
Angle
Which way the camera is aimed, in degrees left(-)/right(+) of straight forward — its "toe-in" or "toe-out."
Field of View (FOV)
The full angular width of what the camera can see, in degrees. Defaults to 70°, a common wide-angle webcam spec.
View Range
How far out (in inches) cones are drawn and scored — a stand-in for your camera's effective detection distance.
Overlap Area
Share of all covered ground that's seen by two or more cameras at once.
Coverage Width
Total left-to-right span covered by any camera at your view range's far edge.
Blind Gap
The widest uncovered stretch between two cones, sampled at roughly two-thirds of your view range. "None" means the cones fully merge by that depth.
