What each camera actually sees

Each panel below is a simplified frame for that camera: AprilTag targets within its cone are drawn at the correct bearing and at their true angular size, and a gold band marks the slice of the frame that another camera's cone also reaches at your Target Distance. A gold ring around a tag means that specific target is visible to more than one camera — useful when deciding which camera should be the "primary" for a given target. A solid dark line marks Target Distance itself.

Overview

Why camera placement matters

A single camera on a robot only sees a wedge of the field — how wide that wedge is depends on its field of view (FOV), and how far it reaches depends on its resolution, how aggressively its AprilTag pipeline decimates frames, and the tag it's trying to read. Adding a second (or third) camera can widen your coverage, cover a blind spot, or give you redundant eyes on the same target — but only if you place it correctly. Mount two cameras too close together and pointed the same way and you've basically built one camera twice; toe them outward and you can lose the overlap you need for hand-off between cameras entirely.

Objective

Design a layout, then stress-test it

Start from the default two-camera rig and drag the Target Distance slider out — watch Overlap shrink as the cones separate with range. Then increase Decimation in the Camera Model panel and see every camera's estimated range collapse, since the AprilTag pipeline is now working from a lower effective resolution. Toe the cameras outward to grow Coverage Width at the cost of a near-field Blind Gap, or inward to close that gap but give up width. Drop a few AprilTag targets onto the field to see exactly which ones fall in the overlap zone. Switch to 360° mode to place cameras anywhere around a square chassis with full rotational freedom and cover the space behind the robot too.

Reference

Controls & terms

Position (X / Y)
Where the camera is mounted, in inches from the robot's center. Baseline mode only edits X (left/right); 360° mode edits both.
Angle
Which way the camera is aimed, in degrees clockwise from straight forward — its "toe-in" or "toe-out." Full ±180° in 360° mode.
Field of View (FOV)
The full angular width of what the camera can see, in degrees. Defaults to 70°, a common wide-angle spec.
Resolution / Decimation
Your camera's native pixel resolution, and how much the AprilTag pipeline downsamples each frame before detecting tags — higher decimation is faster but shortens detection range.
Est. Detection Range
The distance at which this camera's own FOV/resolution/decimation, against the configured AprilTag size, no longer gives enough pixels across the tag to reliably detect it. A simplified geometric estimate, not a guarantee.
Target Distance
The depth at which Coverage, Overlap, and Blind Gap are evaluated — and the depth the sample-view overlap bands are computed against.
Overlap
Of the ground covered by any camera at Target Distance, the share also covered by a second (or third) camera.
Coverage Width / Arc
Baseline mode: total left-to-right span covered at Target Distance. 360° mode: the covered slice of the full circle around the robot.
Blind Gap
The widest uncovered stretch between two cones at Target Distance. "None" means the cones fully merge there.
Targets
Standard-size AprilTags you place by clicking the top-down view — click an existing one to remove it, or use Shuffle/Clear.
