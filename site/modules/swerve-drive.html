Swerve Drive

Every module on a swerve chassis independently steers and drives, so unlike mecanum there's no fixed roller angle to do the mixing for you — each module has to compute its own target angle and speed from the chassis's desired motion. Write a Java step(dt) that calls setModuleState(i, angle, speed) for all four modules. Steering motors are inertia-aware here too — torque limits, max speed, and damping all push back, so a module commanded to spin 170° takes real time to get there.

Objective

Give every module its own angle and speed

Each module sits at a fixed offset (mx, my) from the chassis center. Its required velocity vector is the chassis's translation plus the extra velocity that rotation adds at that offset: vx = driveX − rotate·my, vy = driveY + rotate·mx. The module's target angle is the direction of that vector, and its speed is its magnitude. Because steering motors have inertia, a module asked to swing 170° takes real time to get there — real swerve code (WPILib's SwerveModuleState.optimize) avoids that by checking whether flipping the wheel 180° and reversing its drive direction reaches the same net motion with less than 90° of rotation. Compare Direct Steering against Optimized Steering with a hard rotation reversal on the joystick and watch how much faster the optimized version settles.
